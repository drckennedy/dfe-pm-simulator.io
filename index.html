Briefing · Design for Environment

Build a drone that doesn't cost the Earth.

You are the lead product manager at Aurora Systems, a startup commercializing an autonomous environmental survey drone for scientific fieldwork, agriculture, and port monitoring. Over the next twenty minutes or so, you will drive this product from planning to production — making a sequence of design decisions that will shape its carbon footprint, material chemistry, end-of-life fate, unit cost, and market reception.

Fig. 1 — Product Architecture, Baseline

The Assignment

The global drone market has grown past the point where materials and energy choices are invisible. A commercial quadcopter contains aluminum, carbon-fiber composites, rare-earth magnets, lithium cells, and a dozen plastics — most of which end their lives in landfills or incinerators. Your job is to change that.

Aurora-1 must ship in twelve months. It must compete on capability with incumbents from DJI and Parrot, but it must also earn EPEAT Gold and Cradle-to-Cradle Bronze certification. You have a fixed budget and a board that still cares about margin. Every choice trades off against every other.

The Four DFE Rules

01
Circulate industrial materials

Design so every technical material can re-enter manufacturing indefinitely.

02
Return natural materials

Any bio-based component must be safely returnable to nature's cycles.

03
Eliminate toxics

No "molecular garbage" — no substance that neither cycle can metabolize.

04
Run on clean energy

Renewables for production; design for low in-use energy intensity.

How It Works

The simulation is organized into several rounds, each matching a phase of the standard product development process: Planning, Concept, System & Detail Design, and Production & End-of-Life.

In each round you will make a series of decisions. Each decision carries plausible consequences drawn from current industry research on UAV sustainability (MDPI 2025 LCA reviews, the European LIFE DRONE battery project, Herman Miller's C2C framework, and Ulrich & Eppinger Ch. 12).

The Coherence Penalty

A DFE program is judged on its weakest link. Each decision flagged as anti-DFE in the post-brief compounds a penalty against your final score (up to −45% for a fully incoherent program). Heroic choices on some axes cannot rescue a contradiction elsewhere.

Supplemental Brief

Two Life Cycles, One Product.

OPTIONAL READING

Biological vs. Industrial

McDonough and Braungart argue that every material in a product belongs to one of two metabolisms. The biological cycle absorbs organic compounds back into soil, water, and life. The industrial cycle keeps synthetic materials circulating among manufacturers forever, with no loss of quality.

A cradle-to-cradle product makes the choice explicit: every gram of material has a declared home. Anything that fits neither cycle — a mixed-polymer laminate, a flame-retarded foam, a glued-on coating — is molecular garbage, and the design is not yet finished.

For drones, this framing is brutal. The quickest path to lightness is carbon-fiber composites set in thermoset resins, which are essentially un-recyclable. Bio-based flax-fiber composites and thermoplastic CFRP (recyclable via re-melting) change that equation.

Where Impact Hides

Ulrich & Eppinger distinguish two product archetypes:

  • Materials-intensive products (a laptop) concentrate impact in extraction, processing, and end-of-life.
  • Use-intensive products (a car) concentrate impact in the operating phase.

A survey drone is a hybrid: its frame and battery dominate embodied impact, while its energy use matters when it is flown thousands of hours per year. This simulation makes you weigh both.

The DFE Process

Embed environment into the existing product development flow. Per Ulrich & Eppinger, this is a seven-step loop:

  1. Set the DFE agenda during Planning.
  2. Identify potential environmental impacts.
  3. Select material & DFE guidelines.
  4. Apply the guidelines to initial designs.
  5. Assess impact with LCA tools (SimaPro, GaBi, OpenLCA, SolidWorks Sustainability).
  6. Refine, then loop.
  7. Reflect after shipping.

The simulation's rounds compress this loop. Early rounds cover agenda-setting, impact identification, concept development, and material selection. Later rounds move into system-level guideline application, detail design, production, logistics, and end-of-life — closing the loop.

The Benchmarks

A well-designed drone in this simulation will beat its prior attempt on at least four metrics. A great one will achieve ≥ 75% on Recyclability (Herman Miller's Setu chair reaches 92%), ≥ 50% on Material Chemistry safety, and finish with positive Environmental and Market scores — without losing the Cost battle.

Beware the heroic material.

A single "green" choice rarely saves a product. A bio-based frame paired with an unrecyclable battery and no take-back program is not circular — it's a marketing slogan. The score penalizes incoherent stacks.
